Since **Royal Decree 933/2021** made electronic submission of guest reports through SES.HOSPEDAJES mandatory, many agencies and owners ask themselves the same thing: **what exactly happens if I don't comply?** The short answer is that penalties can range from a few hundred euros to several hundred thousand, depending on severity. The long answer requires understanding how infractions are classified and in which cases each bracket applies.

In this article we review the applicable enforcement framework, the amounts set by Organic Law 4/2015 on Public Safety (Ley Orgánica 4/2015), and how to minimize operational risk.

## Where do the penalties come from?

RD 933/2021 itself does not set the fines. What it does is establish the **reporting obligation** and refer to the general enforcement regime for public safety. That regime is governed by **Organic Law 4/2015, of March 30, on Public Safety** (Ley Orgánica 4/2015), popularly known as the "Gag Law" (Ley Mordaza), which classifies infractions into three levels.

Article **37.3** of Organic Law 4/2015 expressly typifies as a minor infraction:

> *"The omission or inaccuracy of the communication to the Registry of the data referred to in the public safety regulations in relation to travelers and persons lodged in lodging establishments."*

This means that, in principle, failing to submit the guest report falls under **minor infractions**. But that doesn't cover the full picture: repetition, the volume of failures, or the willful alteration of data can raise the classification to serious or very serious, with much larger amounts.

## Amounts by bracket

Organic Law 4/2015 establishes three fine brackets:

| Severity | Amount |
|----------|---------|
| **Minor** | From **€100 to €600** |
| **Serious** | From **€601 to €30,000** |
| **Very serious** | From **€30,001 to €600,000** |

Within each bracket, there are also **degrees** (minimum, medium, and maximum) that the Administration applies based on circumstances such as intent, damage caused, the offender's economic capacity, or repetition.

### Minor infraction (€100–€600)

Applies by default for failing to submit a guest report or submitting it with errors. In practice, if an accommodation fails to report an isolated stay and promptly rectifies it, the amount usually falls in the lower end of the range.

### Serious infraction (€601–€30,000)

A minor infraction can escalate to serious when several circumstances converge:

- **Repetition**: several failures not corrected over time.
- **Significant volume**: failing to report tens or hundreds of stays.
- **Deliberate concealment**: not only omitting, but altering data to avoid reporting.
- **Obstruction** of the inspection work of the Security Forces.

### Very serious infraction (€30,001–€600,000)

The very serious brackets are designed for repeated willful conduct with serious impact on public safety. It is not the usual scenario for a tourist accommodation complying in good faith, but it can arise in cases of accommodations that systematically operate outside the registry.

## Who sanctions and how it is processed

Enforcement authority rests with the **Spanish Ministry of the Interior** through the **Directorate-General of the Police** and the **Directorate-General of the Guardia Civil**, depending on the territorial scope. In Catalonia the competent **regional Ministry** acts in coordination with the **Mossos d'Esquadra**, and in the Basque Country the **Department of Security** of the Basque Government with the **Ertzaintza**.

The general sanctioning procedure includes:

1. **Inspection report or complaint** (which can originate from cross-referencing SES.HOSPEDAJES data with other official registries, not only from an on-site inspection).
2. **Proposed resolution** with the classification and amount.
3. **Allegations period** in which the accommodation can provide evidence and acknowledge or challenge the facts.
4. **Resolution** that ends the administrative process.

Voluntary acknowledgment and early payment can lead to **reductions of 20%** on the amount, according to the general rules of the administrative sanctioning procedure.

## Most common scenarios that end in penalties

These are the scenarios we see most often among accommodations that contact us after receiving a notice:

### 1. OTA reservations without a submitted report

An apartment with high turnover on Booking.com or Airbnb that manages reservations outside the PMS and forgets to register the travelers. When inspectors cross-reference portal data with SES.HOSPEDAJES, the gaps come to light all at once.

### 2. Reports submitted past the deadline

The regulation requires the communication at the moment of check-in or within the **24 hours** following the start of the lodging. Sending it weeks later, even if it is sent, is an untimely communication and can be penalized.

### 3. Incomplete or incorrect data

Common errors: missing **second surname** on Spanish documents, missing **support number** on the DNI, **relationship** not declared for minors aged 14 to 17, or dates of birth inconsistent with the document type.

### 4. Cancellation not reported

If a reservation is canceled after the lodging reservation has been submitted to the Ministry and the **cancellation** is not reported, the system considers that the accommodation had unregistered guests.

### 5. Multi-property without registering all establishments

Each property needs its own **establishment code**. If you manage several and only register one, the rest are outside the registry.

## How to minimize risk

The safest way to comply is not to remember to do it manually, but to **automate** the process end to end. A robust flow rests on three pillars:

**1. Structured data capture.** Have the guest fill in their data in a digital form that validates required fields in real time before allowing them to continue. No blank fields, no impossible dates, no badly formatted documents.

**2. Automatic submission to SES.HOSPEDAJES.** No manual steps that can be forgotten, with submission confirmation and a record of the Ministry's response. If submission fails, immediate alerts so you can fix it before the 24-hour deadline closes.

**3. Full traceability.** An auditable log of every communication: what was sent, when, with what batch code, and what the response was. In a potential inspection, this is gold: it demonstrates diligence and reduces the classification.
